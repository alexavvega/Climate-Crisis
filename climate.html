Climate Crisis Awareness

1. Monarch Populations Have Plummeted

Over the past few decades, migratory monarch butterfly populations have seen devastating declines. The western population has dropped by 99.9%, from around 10 million butterflies in the 1980s to fewer than 2,000 in 2021. In the east, the population has fallen by 84% since 1996. These numbers reflect more than just a species in trouble—they point to a broader ecological imbalance driven by human activity. Without immediate action, monarchs could disappear entirely from the wild.

2. Climate Change Disrupts Migration Patterns

Monarch butterflies rely on environmental cues like temperature and daylight to guide their migration. However, climate change has caused unpredictable weather patterns, leading some butterflies to migrate earlier or farther than normal. These mistimed migrations often result in butterflies arriving before milkweed—their essential food source—is available. Prolonged droughts and severe storms also destroy habitats and directly kill millions of monarchs. These disruptions can lead to reproductive failure and weaken the overall population year after year.

3. Milkweed: Both Lifeline and Risk

Milkweed is the only plant monarch caterpillars eat, making it absolutely essential for their survival. Unfortunately, this vital plant is under threat from climate change and pesticide use. As temperatures rise, milkweed produces more cardenolide, a toxin that protects it from predators but can also harm monarch larvae. Habitat loss from urbanization and agriculture has also led to a massive decline in milkweed availability across North America. Without enough healthy milkweed, monarch butterflies cannot successfully reproduce.

4. Habitat Loss from Fires and Farming

The monarch’s overwintering forests in Mexico and California are being destroyed by logging, agriculture, and wildfires, many of which are made worse by climate change. These areas provide shelter during cold months, and without them, monarchs cannot survive the winter. Deforestation—both legal and illegal—has already wiped out large portions of these critical habitats. Urban sprawl and pesticide-heavy farming continue to push the butterflies out of their breeding and resting areas. This ongoing loss of shelter makes long-term survival incredibly difficult.

5. Monarchs Help Feed the Planet

Monarch butterflies are part of a larger network of pollinators that support ecosystems and agriculture. Alongside bees, bats, and birds, monarchs help fertilize plants by transferring pollen, enabling fruits, vegetables, and flowers to grow. As their populations decline, the ripple effects can be felt across food systems and biodiversity. If monarchs migrate too far north due to warmer springs, they may not survive the longer return trip south. This strain reduces reproduction and weakens pollination cycles across North America.

6. Hope Through Action

Despite the alarming decline in monarch butterfly numbers, there is hope. Communities, scientists, and conservationists are coming together to plant native milkweed, reduce pesticide use, and restore natural habitats. By supporting efforts to protect overwintering forests and creating pollinator-friendly gardens, individuals can help monarchs recover. These small but powerful actions can help reverse the butterfly’s path toward extinction. Together, we can ensure future generations experience the magic of monarch migration.
